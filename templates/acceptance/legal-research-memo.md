# Acceptance Criteria: Legal Research Memo

Copy this file to `.quiver/acceptance.md` in your workspace to define what a
complete, high-quality legal research memo looks like. The maker-checker will
verify each criterion before the agent writes the final deliverable.

## Structure
- [ ] Memo has a clear title, date, and author/prepared-by line
- [ ] Memo states the legal question(s) being researched
- [ ] Memo has a short answer / conclusion section
- [ ] Memo has a detailed analysis section
- [ ] Memo has a sources / authorities section

## Legal Analysis
- [ ] Each legal claim cites the specific statute, regulation, or case law
- [ ] Case citations include full citation format (e.g., Smith v. Jones, 123 F.3d 456 (2d Cir. 2024))
- [ ] Statutory citations include the specific section and subsection
- [ ] Both favorable and unfavorable authorities are addressed
- [ ] Analysis distinguishes between settled law and open questions

## Provenance
- [ ] Every legal proposition has an inline citation
- [ ] No case or statute is cited without a verifiable source
- [ ] Sources are primary (actual statutes, case opinions) not secondary commentary
- [ ] Where secondary sources are used, they are clearly labeled as such
- [ ] The memo explicitly states when a claim is based on the agent's analysis vs. direct citation

## Caveats
- [ ] Memo includes a disclaimer that it is not legal advice
- [ ] Memo notes jurisdictional limitations (e.g., "this analysis applies to US federal law")
- [ ] Memo identifies areas where further research by a qualified attorney is needed

## Format
- [ ] Memo uses formal legal writing style
- [ ] No placeholder text (TODO, FIXME, lorem ipsum)
- [ ] No unexplained legal jargon (define on first use)
- [ ] Minimum 1500 words for substantive legal research
